The first thing you see on a Canberra site after the survey pegs go in is usually a compact excavator with a rock bucket or a set of hand augers. That’s the start of a proper raft foundation investigation, and around here it almost always means exposing the stiff residual clays that make the ACT’s geology both unique and unforgiving. The rigs we mobilise—typically a 5-tonne excavator for test pits down to 2.5 metres or a DCP rig for in-situ penetration—let us map the reactivity depth before the structural engineer even thinks about rib spacing. In suburbs like Belconnen or Tuggeranong, where natural ground can swing from dry hardpan to saturated plastic clay within a few hundred metres, the physical inspection of the founding material is non-negotiable. We pair that site observation with a CPT test program whenever the profile shows layered silts that need continuous tip resistance and sleeve friction data to finalise the slab stiffness parameters.

Canberra’s reactive clays can generate over 50 mm of differential movement across a standard house footprint—a raft slab designed to AS 2870 turns that movement into a manageable engineering parameter rather than a structural failure.

Method and coverage

AS 2870:2011 is the bible for residential slabs and footings in Australia, and in Canberra it carries more weight than in most other capitals. The reason is simple: the city sits on deeply weathered Ordovician and Silurian metasediments overlain by reactive clays that can exhibit characteristic surface movements of 40 to 75 mm under seasonal moisture variation. That puts most of the ACT squarely into Class M to H1 site classification territory, and a growing number of infill developments in older suburbs like Ainslie or Narrabundah push into H2 once trees or leaking services are factored in. Our raft designs account for these edge heave and centre heave scenarios by adjusting beam depth, slab thickness, and reinforcement layout to the specific ys value derived from shrink-swell testing. We run Atterberg limits, linear shrinkage, and suction profiles on undisturbed tube samples, then feed the results into a finite element model that verifies deflection limits under AS 3600 serviceability criteria. For sites with uncontrolled fill or deep soft spots, we often recommend ground improvement through vibrocompaction before finalising the raft geometry, which reduces differential settlement risk in the long term.

Regional considerations

Canberra recorded a population of just over 470,000 in the 2023 census estimates, and that number keeps climbing—which means more construction on marginal blocks that were passed over decades ago. Those leftover lots frequently sit on deep colluvium or poorly compacted fill, and putting a standard waffle raft on them without a detailed geotechnical investigation is a gamble that backfires when the first dry spell hits. The 2021 Molonglo Valley subsidence incidents were a wake-up call: several properties experienced cracking within two years of handover because the raft design assumed a uniform clay profile that simply wasn’t there. We’ve learned to be aggressive with site investigation density—minimum one borehole per 200 square metres on Class H sites—and to specify deeper edge beams where mature eucalypts are within the zone of influence. The extra upfront cost of a properly designed raft is trivial compared to the legal and reputational cost of underpinning a cracked slab later on.

Q&A

How much does a raft foundation design cost for a standard residential block in Canberra?

For a typical single-dwelling residential site in the ACT, a complete raft foundation design package—including site classification testing, engineering calculations, and stamped drawings—generally falls between AU$1,420 and AU$5,680. The range depends on site access, the number of boreholes required, and whether the reactivity class triggers additional testing like soil suction profiles or tree influence assessments.

Is a raft slab better than a strip footing system for Canberra's reactive clays?

In most cases, yes. Raft slabs distribute structural loads across the entire footprint, which helps bridge localised soft spots and reduces differential movement compared to isolated strip footings. For Class M and H sites—which cover the majority of Canberra's residential land—a stiffened raft is the standard solution under AS 2870, and it generally performs better over the long term when seasonal moisture changes cause the clay to shrink and swell.

What depth of site investigation do you need before designing a raft foundation?

We follow AS 1726 and AS 2870 guidelines, which for a typical Canberra site means advancing boreholes to at least the depth of suction change—usually between 1.8 and 3.0 metres in the ACT's clay profiles. On sites with fill or deep weathering, boreholes may extend to 4 or 5 metres, and we'll retrieve undisturbed samples at multiple depths to capture the full shrink-swell profile.

How long does the raft foundation design process take from start to finish?

A typical turnaround from site investigation to final issued-for-construction drawings is around two to three weeks. The field work and lab testing for reactivity parameters takes about one week, and the engineering analysis and drafting takes another week to ten days, depending on the complexity of the slab geometry and any peer review requirements.
