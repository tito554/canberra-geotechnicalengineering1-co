Electrical Resistivity Surveys & VES for Ground Investigation in Canberra

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

EXPLORE →

Canberra's landscape is deceptive. Beneath the orderly suburbs and bushland reserves, the geology shifts abruptly from deeply weathered Ordovician metasediments to fresh ignimbrite and limestone lenses, often within a single site. In our experience, conventional boreholes miss these lateral transitions unless they happen to intersect a contact. That is where electrical resistivity methods, particularly Vertical Electrical Sounding and 2D profiling, become indispensable. We regularly deploy dipole-dipole and Schlumberger arrays across the Molonglo Valley and Jerrabomberra wetlands, where the water table sits high and saline dispersions create distinct resistivity contrasts. When a developer needs to know whether that low-resistivity zone is saturated fill or natural alluvium, a targeted VES survey provides the answer without extensive trenching. For projects where soil stiffness is also in question, pairing resistivity with a seismic refraction survey often resolves ambiguity between weathered rock and dense granular layers.

A well-executed VES profile in Canberra's weathered metasediments can distinguish fresh rock from highly weathered zones without a single borehole — but only if the electrode coupling survives a 35-degree summer day.

Method and coverage

The ACT's climate adds another layer of complexity to resistivity measurements. Canberra endures a marked dry-summer continental pattern, with frosty winters that can saturate near-surface soils and parched summers that drive electrical contact resistance sky-high. A technician who only reads the manual will spend half the morning troubleshooting electrode coupling in February when the top 300 mm of clay has baked to brick hardness. Our field crews pre-soak electrode positions and use bentonite paste at each stake, a small habit that prevents a 40-minute line from returning noisy, uninterpretable data. We also adapt electrode spacing to the local geology: on the Ainslie Volcanics, where fresh dacite can appear within 2 metres of surface, we tighten the a-spacing to capture the rapid resistivity rise before the signal attenuates. The resulting pseudosections map fracture zones and weathering fronts with enough detail to plan exploratory drillholes. In the softer sediments of the Gungahlin plain, wider spacings reveal the undulating bedrock interface that governs foundation design. Where shallow groundwater and clays complicate interpretation, a grain-size analysis from a few calibration pits helps constrain the saturation model used during inversion.

Regional considerations

AS 1726-2017 requires that the ground investigation be sufficient to characterise the site's geological model, and the ACT Planning and Land Authority increasingly expects geophysical evidence to support borehole data in complex ground. The risk of skipping a resistivity survey here is not hypothetical: we have seen projects on the Canberra Formation where isolated boreholes indicated competent rock, yet a 2D resistivity line later revealed a 15-metre-wide paleochannel filled with soft, saturated clay directly beneath the proposed footing line. That kind of surprise leads to redesigns, variation claims, and six-month delays. The geotechnical risk is even sharper on the western side of Lake Burley Griffin, where fill thickness can vary from 0.5 to 8 metres over short distances. Resistivity imaging is one of the few methods that can map that fill-rock interface continuously, which is why we recommend it as part of any desk study before major excavation.

Complementary services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Single-point Schlumberger array soundings to model layered stratigraphy, ideal for estimating bedrock depth and groundwater table position beneath a proposed footing or borehole.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiling along transects up to 400 metres, producing continuous cross-sections that reveal lateral changes in lithology, fracture zones, and fill boundaries.

03

Time-lapse resistivity monitoring

Repeated 2D surveys to track seasonal groundwater movement, contaminant plume evolution, or changes in soil moisture beneath engineered fill.

04

Resistivity-borehole correlation

Integrated reporting that overlays resistivity profiles onto borehole logs, calibrating geophysical boundaries against recovered core and SPT data for a unified ground model.

Q&A

What depth can a VES survey reach in Canberra's geology?

A typical Schlumberger VES with a maximum current electrode half-spacing (AB/2) of 100 metres can investigate to roughly 50-70 metres depth in the weathered metasediments of the Canberra Formation. The actual penetration depends on the resistivity of the near-surface layers; highly conductive clays in the Jerrabomberra area may attenuate the signal and reduce effective depth, while dry sands and gravels on the higher terraces allow deeper current penetration.

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in the ACT?

For a single VES sounding or a short 2D profile up to 100 metres, costs typically range from AU$910 to AU$1,390, depending on site access, electrode spacing requirements, and the number of cable spreads needed. Larger multi-line surveys are quoted based on total lineal metres and the complexity of the terrain model.

Can resistivity distinguish between dry fill and natural ground?

Yes, in most cases. Fill materials in Canberra, particularly those sourced from weathered volcanics or construction rubble, tend to exhibit heterogeneous resistivity signatures with sharp lateral variations, whereas natural alluvial or residual soils show more gradual, stratigraphically coherent transitions. We calibrate these boundaries against test pit observations wherever possible to increase confidence in the interpretation.
